Intro

In this project, you will find my texts written in Iceland between 2017 and 2025. Some lived quietly in my Mac, others reached a few people who were close to me. Now I am giving them back, because everything I learned there, I can only repay by setting these words free.

Prologue

Icelandic landscape has incredible healing potential. Every time I observe its dynamic changes, how the earth and the weather conduct their never ending dialogue, I feel as if I am observing myself from the inside. The part that is hidden from ordinary sight. The part you cannot really convey to anyone, and even if you try to tell someone how you feel, they will never experience it the same way.

Stand up comedian Jirka Charvát captured this quite aptly in one of his performances. He says that each of us is basically a little pickle in our own private jar, and when we try to get closer to each other, we only end up desperately bumping glass against glass. Our experiences may not be transferable, but we can share them. Just as we share the landscape and the environment we live in.

People who choose to live on this remote rock in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean fascinate me just as much as the island itself. The desire for a shared external landscape often hints at a similarity in the internal one. At least that is what I have observed and heard from the people I met on my travels. Some of them eventually became close friends, others remained just Facebook acquaintances, and a few turned into friends for life. And so you slowly build your own little tribe, a circle of like minded souls.

In a tiny village on the western edge of the Westfjords, a framed picture hangs at the reception of a guesthouse with the text: Friends are like stars. You may not see them for a long time, yet you know they are there somewhere. This is doubly true for friendships formed in Iceland. Thanks to the polar day, you really cannot see the stars in the sky during the summer. At most, you might catch the pale outline of the moon here and there.

I remember that when I spent my first days on the island, I thought the moon did not even rise here. It was cloudy, and I was looking for it on the wrong side of the sky. Then, one night in September, it finally appeared and I felt strangely happy that it was there with me too.

A few days later, my better half woke me up in the middle of the night. “Wake up, come on, wake up already. You just have to see this.” When I reached the window, sleepy and grumpy, my jaw dropped instantly. A soft pink aurora borealis danced across the entire sky. She danced above a swan shaped evangelical church, over mountain tops, over fjords and over the ocean. I had never seen anything like it in my life, and it felt unlikely I ever would again. When you expect the aurora to be green, pink is a shock. And definitely a blessing.

It was a confirmation for me that when you are in the right place at the right time, the universe shows you certain signs. Sometimes it is worth overcoming fears and anxieties, lifting yourself off the ground and flying somewhere. Or leaving something, or someone, behind.

Iceland constantly puts me in situations where letting go becomes a necessary step forward. It teaches me not to cling, to trust that I am always taken care of, everywhere, and that worry has no real purpose. And even though it is sometimes hard, I try to keep my heart open to whatever has to come next.

"To see the world, things dangerous to come to. To see behind walls, to draw closer. To find each other and to feel. That is the purpose of life." - The Secret Life of Walter Mitty
